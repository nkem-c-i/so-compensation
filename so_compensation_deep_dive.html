1. Introduction & Goal

Project Title:
Compensation Deep Dive: StackOverflow Developer Survey 2024

Context & Motivation:
The StackOverflow Annual Developer Survey collects detailed information about developers backgrounds, skills, and compensation from around the world. Understanding compensation trends helps professionals benchmark their own earnings, informs recruiters about market rates, and highlights geographic or demographic disparities in pay. In this analysis, we will focus on ConvertedCompYearlythe yearly compensation (in USD) reported by respondentsand explore how it varies by country, age, education level, and developer role.

Objectives:

  1. Clean and prepare the salary column (ConvertedCompYearly) for analysis, including handling missing or invalid values.
  2. Compute and visualize median compensation across the top 10 respondent countries.
  3. Investigate salary distributions by age group and education level.
  4. Analyze compensation for the most common developer roles.
  5. Create clear, interpretable visualizations (boxplots, bar charts, heatmaps) and summarize actionable insights.

Notebook Structure:

  1. Data Loading & Column Verification: Read the survey data and confirm key columns.
  2. Data Cleaning & Transformation: Filter and transform ConvertedCompYearly, bin ages and education, and explode DevType.
  3. Analysis & Visualization: Compute median salaries by country, age group, education, and role; generate boxplots, bar charts, and heatmaps.
  4. Conclusions & Insights: Summarize key findings and discuss potential implications.

3. Data Cleaning & Transformation

Raw survey data often contains missing, invalid, or inconsistent values. In this section, we will:

  1. Filter Salary
    • Remove rows where ConvertedCompYearly is null, zero, or negative.
  2. Transform Salary
    • Create a logtransformed salary column (SalaryLog) to reduce skew.
    • (Optionally) Create a MinMax normalized salary column (SalaryMinMax).
  3. Bin Age
    • Convert Age strings (e.g., 25-34) into ordered AgeGroup categories.
  4. Bucket Education
    • Simplify EdLevel text into broad buckets like NoDegree, Associate, Bachelors, Masters, PostGrad.
  5. Explode Developer Roles
    • Split DevType (semicolonseparated) and .explode() so each row represents one respondentrole.

Below are the code cells for each step.

# 3.4. Bucket EdLevel into broad categories
def simplify_education(level):
    if pd.isna(level):
        return 'NoAnswer'
    lvl = level.lower()
    if 'bachelor' in lvl:
        return 'Bachelors'
    if 'master' in lvl:
        return 'Masters'
    if 'professional' in lvl or 'doctoral' in lvl or 'phd' in lvl:
        return 'PostGrad'
    if 'associate' in lvl:
        return 'Associate'
    if 'no formal education' in lvl:
        return 'NoDegree'
    return 'Other'

df_clean['EdBucket'] = df_clean['EdLevel'].apply(simplify_education)

5. Conclusions & Insights

5.1 Recap of Key Findings

TopPaying Countries:
The United States and Canada reported the highest median yearly compensation among all respondents. Developers in the U.S. earn some of the top salaries globally, followed closely by their counterparts in Canada.

Age Factor:
Respondents aged 4554 reported the highest median compensation, with a notable jump in salary occurring after approximately 11 years of professional coding experience.

Education Premium:
Postgraduate degree holders (Masters, PhD, or professional degrees) reported the highest median salary (~$80k), compared to those with a Bachelor's degree (~$67k) and those without a formal degree (~$50k). This supports a modest but clear return on investment for advanced education in tech.

Role Variation:
Among all roles reported, Engineering Managers and respondents who selected "Other (please specify)" had the highest median compensation. This may reflect the wide range of senior or niche roles that fall into these categories.

5.2 Contextual Interpretation

Regional Differences:
Higher salaries in the U.S. and Canada reflect both strong tech sector demand and higher costs of living. These regions also tend to attract or retain more senior developers.

Experience Premium:
The compensation jump after 11 years of professional experience suggests that long-term industry knowledge and leadership roles command significantly higher pay.

Educational Return:
While a formal degree isnt a strict requirement for high pay in tech, advanced degrees still correlate with higher compensation, especially in managerial or specialized roles.

Role Definitions:
Engineering Managers likely reflect leadership positions with broader responsibilities, while Other may include specialized or executive roles not captured by predefined survey categories.

5.3 Business & Career Implications

For Job Seekers:
If aiming for top-tier compensation, consider growing into leadership roles like Engineering Management and developing specialized skillsets. Gaining 10+ years of experience appears to significantly impact earnings potential.

For Hiring Managers:
Benchmark salaries in the U.S. and Canada at a premium and be prepared to offer competitive packages for experienced professionals or individuals with postgraduate degrees.

For Educators/Bootcamps:
Encourage pathways toward postgraduate or leadership education for those targeting higher-earning roles. The data shows a meaningful correlation between formal education and compensation.

These insights are useful for job seekers, hiring teams, and educators who want to understand or respond to current compensation trends.
